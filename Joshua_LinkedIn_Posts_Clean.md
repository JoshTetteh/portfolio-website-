### Post 01
**Theme: Why data projects fail**
Nobody's dashboard ever got shelved because the data was wrong.
Most data projects in Ghana don't fail just because of bad data.
They fail because the person who commissioned the dashboard doesn't trust the numbers.
So they call a meeting to "validate" it. Then another to "review the methodology." Then it gets shelved because quarters changed and priorities shifted.
The data was fine. The culture wasn't ready for what the data was saying.
This is the thing nobody talks about in all the "digital transformation" conversations happening in boardrooms right now.
Tools are the easy part. Behaviour is the hard part.
Have you ever watched a good initiative die in a validation meeting? What actually killed it?

### Post 02
**Theme: AI hype in Africa**
The hottest AI advice online was not written for you.
Everyone in Africa is talking about AI.
Half of them are selling courses. A quarter are writing LinkedIn posts about it (myself included). Very few are actually building things that solve African problems.
Most AI content we consume is built around American or European workflows, salaries, and problems. Then we try to apply it here… and wonder why it doesn't stick.
The context is different. The constraints are different. The problems are different.
A tool that automates proposal writing for a $10M agency in London needs serious rethinking before it works for a 3-person consultancy in Accra.
Context isn't a footnote. It's everything.
What's one piece of AI advice you've tried that clearly wasn't built for how we work here?

### Post 03
**Theme: Who actually fears AI**
The professionals most afraid of AI aren't the ones who don't understand it.
They're the ones whose entire value has been "I know how to find the information."
Google already disrupted that. AI is finishing the job.
But here's what I don't see people saying:
The people who will thrive aren't the ones who resist it or blindly embrace it.
They're the ones who combine their deep domain knowledge, the context, the relationships, the judgment, with what AI can actually do.
Imagine a doctor who knows AI can suggest a diagnosis but can't read a patient's face.
An M&E officer who knows AI can generate a report but can't explain to a district director why the numbers look wrong.
What's the skill you're most confident no tool can take from you?

### Post 04
**Theme: Data frequency & decision speed**
Thirty days is too long to find out something went wrong.
A company I worked with was reviewing their revenue performance once a month.
Which meant when something went wrong, they found out 30 days later. By which time, the person responsible had moved on, the context was gone, and the meeting became a blame session instead of a fix session.
We asked one question: "What if you could see this every hour?"
Same data. Same team. Same systems, mostly.
But when you can see a problem at 10am, you fix it by noon. When you see it on the last Friday of the month, you write a report about it.
The insight isn't about technology. It's about the relationship between data frequency and decision speed.
Every organisation has a "monthly report" that should be a live dashboard.
What's yours?

### Post 05
**Theme: What M&E actually looks like on the ground**
Nobody told me the job was mostly about trust.
When I started working in monitoring and evaluation, I thought the job was building frameworks and tracking KPIs.
It's not.
It's spending 40 minutes convincing a district officer that the data he submitted doesn't add up, without making him feel attacked, so that next month he actually sends accurate numbers instead of numbers he thinks you want to see.
It's designing a template simple enough that someone fills it out in a moving car with bad network.
It's understanding that the "data problem" is often a trust problem, a workflow problem, or a "nobody explained why this matters" problem.
The technical skills are a small part of the job. The human skills are everything.
I think about this a lot when people talk about AI replacing analysts.
What's the most human part of your job that no tool could ever replicate?

### Post 06
**Theme: From bureaucracy to building AI systems**
Working inside a big institution was some of the best training I ever had.
I spent years inside a large public institution, learning how organisations make decisions, where the real bottlenecks hide, and why well-intentioned initiatives die in committee.
Then I moved to building AI-powered systems for businesses.
And I realised: the problems are the same.
A digital agency drowning in proposal requests and a government health program struggling to find grants, both have the same underlying issue.
Too much to do. Too little time. Processes that haven't caught up with the volume.
Understanding institutions from the inside changed how I build solutions. I stopped designing for ideal conditions and started designing for Monday morning, with bad WiFi, and a team that's already overwhelmed.
That's when things actually get used.
Which environment taught you more, a structured institution or a fast, scrappy one?

### Post 07
**Theme: How to actually start using AI at work**
Everyone tries AI the wrong way the first time.
Most people try it, get a mediocre result, and conclude it doesn't work.
The real issue: they started with the wrong task.
Don't start with strategy. Don't start with creativity.
Start with your most predictable, repetitive, text-heavy task.
For most professionals it's one of these:, Writing the first draft of a report you write every month, Summarising meeting notes into action points, Turning bullet points into a professional email
Pick one. Just one.
Spend a week getting good at prompting it, giving it context, format, and examples.
By week 2, that task is no longer costing you 2 hours. It's costing you 20 minutes.
Now you have headspace for the harder problems.
That's how the shift actually happens. Not in a workshop. Not in a revelation. In one small, boring task done better.
What's the first task you'd hand over if you knew it would actually work?

### Post 08
**Theme: What KPIs are actually for**
If nobody acts on it between reports, it isn't a KPI. It's a ritual.
A way of appearing organised without actually being responsive.
The purpose of a performance indicator is to trigger a decision, not to fill a slide deck.
If your team tracks 40 KPIs every quarter and nothing changes as a result, you don't need more data.
You need fewer metrics and faster loops.
3 metrics you actually act on will do more for performance than 20 that generate a 60-page report read once and archived.
Measurement should make you faster. If it's making you slower, something is wrong with the design.
How many KPIs does your team track? How many actually change what someone does the next day?

### Post 09
**Theme: The soft reveal**
People ask me what I do and I always stumble on the answer.
Because it depends on the week.
Some weeks I'm in a university classroom in Accra explaining to final-year students why learning to prompt an AI well is more career-relevant right now than memorising another framework.
Some weeks I'm debugging a Python script that's supposed to scrape grant databases for a health program that can't afford to miss a funding deadline.
Some weeks I'm sitting with a business owner walking through why their team has data but no insight, and what to do about it.
The thread connecting all of it: organisations and people have more capacity than they're currently using. Usually the gap is a skills gap, a systems gap, or a "nobody has thought clearly about this yet" gap.
That's where I come in.
If any of that sounds like a problem you're living with, my DMs are open.

### Post 10
**Theme: Bad data, not bad culture**
Sometimes the culture is fine and the data is still wrong.
I said before that most data problems are culture problems.
That's true. But let me complicate it.
Sometimes the people are willing. Leadership actually wants to make data-driven decisions.
And the data is still wrong.
Not because of behaviour. Because nobody built the pipes correctly.
A form that double-counts entries. A system where two departments track the same metric differently. A spreadsheet that's been copy-pasted so many times nobody knows which version is the source of truth.
This is a data engineering problem. And in most Ghanaian organisations, there is no one whose job it is to solve it.
We talk endlessly about data analysis. We rarely talk about whether the data being analysed was worth collecting in the first place.
Garbage in. Polished dashboard out. Same garbage.
How confident are you that the numbers in your last report were actually correct?

### Post 11
**Theme: The spreadsheet nobody could explain**
Three people. Same organisation. Same period. Three different numbers.
I once sat in a review meeting where three people had three different figures for the same indicator.
Each person was confident in theirs.
We spent 45 minutes figuring out which one was right. Turns out none of them were, they were each pulling from a different version of a spreadsheet that had been emailed around and edited locally.
No single source of truth. No version control. No data governance.
Just three people, one meeting, and a lot of wasted time.
This is not a rare story. This is Tuesday in most organisations.
The fix isn't a dashboard. It's deciding, once, clearly, with everyone in the room, where the data lives, who owns it, and what the one true version looks like.
That conversation is unsexy. It's also the most important one you'll have.
Has this ever happened in your organisation? How did you resolve it?

### Post 12
**Theme: Data engineering is infrastructure**
You wouldn't blame the interior designer for a building with no electricity.
But organisations do the equivalent with data all the time.
They hire analysts. Buy BI tools. Commission dashboards.
Then wonder why none of it is working.
Because the foundation was never laid.
Data engineering is the plumbing of any data-driven organisation. It's how data gets collected, cleaned, stored, and moved reliably from where it's created to where it's needed.
Without it, your analyst is spending 80% of their time cleaning spreadsheets instead of generating insight.
In most organisations here, this role doesn't exist. It's not even a concept.
Until it is, the dashboards will keep lying.
Does your organisation have someone whose job is specifically data quality, not just data collection?

### Post 13
**Theme: Process is the missing conversation**
AI won't fix a broken process. It will just automate the chaos faster.
We love talking about data. We love talking about AI. We do not like talking about process.
Because process is boring. Process means admitting that the way we've been doing things needs to change. Process means meetings where nobody agrees and decisions that step on toes.
But here's what I keep seeing:
Organisations adopt a new tool, skip the process redesign, and six months later they're doing the same thing they were doing before, just with a shinier interface.
Before you ask "what tool should we use?" ask "do we actually understand how work moves through this organisation?"
Most of the time, that question alone saves you a very expensive mistake.
What's the most broken process in your organisation that everyone knows about but nobody talks about?

### Post 14
**Theme: Audit your own data process in one hour**
You don't need a consultant to know if your data process is broken.
Pick one metric your organisation tracks regularly. Then ask:
, Where does this data come from originally?, How many hands does it pass through before it reaches a report?, Has anyone ever questioned whether it's accurate?, What happens when two people have different numbers?
If you can't answer the first two clearly, data engineering problem. If nobody has ever questioned the accuracy, culture problem. If the fourth question has no agreed answer, process problem.
Most organisations have all three.
The good news is that naming the problem correctly is already half the solution.
Run this on one metric this week and tell me what you find. I'm genuinely curious.

### Post 15
**Theme: Data we collect but never use**
The most common data problem isn't missing data. It's data nobody ever looks at.
The most common data problem I encounter isn't missing data.
It's data that exists, was collected at great effort, and has never once informed a decision.
Forms filled out every week. Reports submitted every month. Databases growing quietly in the background.
And when a decision needs to be made, someone calls a meeting and people speak from opinion and experience because nobody thought to check the records.
Data collection without a clear use case isn't diligence. It's busywork with extra steps.
Before you design another form or add another field, ask: who will look at this, when, and what will they do differently because of it?
If you don't have a clear answer, don't collect it.
What's one thing your organisation collects data on that nobody has ever actually used?

### Post 16
**Theme: When the process breaks the data**
The data wasn't wrong because people were lazy. The process made accuracy impossible.
A team I worked with had field officers submitting reports at the end of every month. By the time they sat down to report, they were reconstructing events from two, three, sometimes four weeks ago, from memory.
The data wasn't lying. People were doing their best.
But the process was designed as if humans have perfect recall and zero competing priorities.
When we moved collection closer to the event, shorter cycles, simpler forms, submitted within 48 hours, accuracy improved dramatically.
Same people. Different process. Better data.
Process design is data strategy. They are not separate conversations.
Where in your workflow is the biggest gap between when something happens and when it gets recorded?

### Post 17
**Theme: Culture isn't soft. It's structural.**
"It's a culture problem" usually means "we gave up trying to fix the system."
When people say culture is the issue they usually mean people need to change their attitude.
That's rarely the real issue.
Culture is just the accumulation of what a system rewards and punishes over time.
If your organisation says data matters but makes decisions based on who spoke loudest in the room, people learn to speak loudly.
If your organisation tracks 30 indicators but only asks about 3 in reviews, people learn to care about 3.
You cannot train your way out of a structural misalignment. A workshop cannot fix incentives.
If you want a data culture, build a system where using data correctly is the path of least resistance.
Everything else is decoration.
What behaviour in your organisation is officially discouraged but quietly rewarded?

### Post 18
**Theme: The question nobody asks before buying a BI tool**
The tool is what you buy after you've done the harder work. Most vendors won't tell you that.
Before your organisation buys a BI tool, analytics platform, or AI system, answer these five questions honestly:
Do we have a single agreed source of truth for our key metrics?
Is someone responsible for data quality, not just data collection?
Do our decision-makers actually trust the data we have right now?
Do we have documented processes for how data moves through the organisation?
Will people genuinely use this, or will it become shelfware in six months?
If you answered no to three or more, the tool is not your problem.
I've watched organisations spend millions on platforms that answered none of these questions first.
Which of these five is the hardest one for your organisation to answer honestly?

### Post 55
**Theme: KPIs nobody actually uses**
The most dangerous indicator in any organisation is one that everyone reports on and nobody acts on.
Most institutions in Ghana have performance frameworks with 20, 30, sometimes 50 indicators. They collect the data every quarter. They compile it into reports. The reports go into folders.
Nobody makes a decision based on them.
The problem is not the indicators. The problem is that the indicators were designed to satisfy a donor or a board, not to answer a question that management actually has.
A useful KPI is one where the answer changes what you do next. If the answer doesn't change anything, it is not a KPI. It is a ritual.
When I was at ECG, we had 30 tracked indicators. Three of them drove every meaningful conversation in the review meetings. The rest were noise dressed up as accountability.
Cutting your indicator list in half is not laziness. It is discipline.
How many indicators in your organisation exist purely because someone asked for them at some point and nobody ever removed them?

### Post 56
**Theme: AI tools vs AI systems**
Using ChatGPT at work is not the same thing as having an AI strategy.
There is a difference between someone on your team using an AI tool individually and your organisation having AI embedded in how work actually gets done.
One is a productivity habit. The other is infrastructure.
Most organisations in Africa are at stage one and calling it stage two. They buy subscriptions, run a workshop, and announce they are now an AI-powered organisation. Meanwhile every core process still runs the same way it did in 2019.
The real shift happens when AI stops being a tool you open separately and starts being something woven into the process itself. A system that generates the first draft of your monthly report automatically. A pipeline that flags anomalies in your data before your analyst sees it. A CRM that scores and routes leads without anyone deciding to do it.
That requires process thinking, not just tool adoption.
Where in your organisation is there a process that runs the same way every time and takes more than two hours to complete?

### Post 57
**Theme: What M&E actually teaches you**
Monitoring and Evaluation is one of the best training grounds for AI work, and almost nobody talks about it that way.
M&E teaches you to ask: what are we trying to measure and why? What counts as evidence? Where does the data come from and how reliable is it? What decision does this information feed into?
Those are exactly the questions you need to answer before you build any AI system.
Most AI implementations fail not because of bad models but because of bad problem definition. The wrong thing gets automated. The output answers a question nobody was actually asking. The data going in was never clean enough to trust.
M&E professionals already know how to interrogate a results framework. They already know the gap between what an indicator says and what is actually happening on the ground.
That instinct is rare and it is valuable. The people who will build the best AI systems in African organisations are often not the engineers. They are the people who have spent years asking whether the numbers tell the real story.
What cross-functional skill in your background do you think is more useful than people realise?

### Post 58
**Theme: Digital transformation is mostly theatre**

Most digital transformation programmes transform the PowerPoint, not the organisation.

A new enterprise system gets procured. There is a launch event. The minister or the CEO gives a speech about the future. Photos are taken.

Six months later, staff are maintaining a parallel manual process because the system does not match how work actually gets done. Or the vendor is unresponsive. Or nobody was trained on edge cases. Or the person who championed it got transferred.

I have watched this happen in large institutions in Ghana more than once.

The issue is that digital transformation gets treated as a procurement exercise when it is actually a change management exercise. The technology is rarely the hard part. Changing how people work, who owns what, and what counts as done is the hard part.

No system survives contact with an organisation that was not ready for it.

What is the biggest gap you have seen between how a digital project was announced and how it actually landed?

### Post 59
**Theme: Why training doesn't stick**
A two-day workshop does not change behaviour. It changes what people say in the room.
Most capacity building in African institutions follows the same pattern. Bring in a facilitator. Participants engage. There is energy. People leave with a certificate and a folder.
Three months later nothing has changed.
The problem is not the training. The problem is what happens after the training. If the system people return to does not reward applying what they learned, they will not apply it. If their manager does not use the new approach, they will not either. If there is no follow-up and no accountability, the workshop becomes a memory.
When I design training programmes now, the question I ask first is not what do participants need to learn. It is what will be different in their work environment six weeks after the training ends. If the answer is nothing, the training is decoration.
Learning sticks when the environment pulls it out of you, not just when an instructor puts it in.
What is one thing you learned in a training that you actually use today, and what made it stick?

### Post 60
**Theme: The hidden cost of manual reporting**
The most expensive line item in your organisation might not be on any budget.
Manual reporting is invisible cost. Someone spends four hours every month pulling data from three different spreadsheets, reconciling discrepancies, formatting a report, and emailing it to twelve people. Multiply that by ten departments. Multiply by twelve months. That is thousands of hours a year spent producing information that could be generated automatically.
And that is before you count the errors. The outdated figures. The version confusion. The decisions made on last month's data because this month's report is still being compiled.
At ECG, one of the first things we tackled was digitising district monitoring processes. The goal was not to look modern. It was to get accurate information faster so management could respond faster. That shift alone changed what was visible and what got acted on.
Before you buy any new tool, map what your current reporting process actually costs in people-hours. The number will usually make the business case for automation faster than any vendor pitch.
How many hours per month does your team spend producing reports that could largely build themselves?

### Post 61
**Theme: The gap between data collection and data use**
Collecting data and using data are two completely different organisational capabilities.
Many institutions in Ghana are excellent at collection. Forms exist. Submissions happen. Databases fill up. Then the data sits there.
No one analyses it between reporting cycles. No one flags when something looks off. The data becomes a compliance mechanism, not a management tool.
The gap is not technical. It is structural. Nobody has been given the time, mandate, or tools to turn that data into a question worth answering. Analysis is treated as a bonus, something you do when there is space, not as a core part of the process.
You do not have a data problem. You have a usage culture problem.
Fixing it means deciding what questions the data should answer, who is responsible for answering them, and on what schedule. That is a governance decision, not a technology purchase.
What data does your organisation collect that nobody actually looks at between reporting periods?

### Post 62
**Theme: What African orgs get wrong about AI adoption**
The AI conversation in Africa is happening at the wrong level.
Most of the discussion is about whether AI is relevant here. Whether the infrastructure is ready. Whether the data is good enough. Whether we should wait for better models.
Meanwhile, organisations with much worse data and much older infrastructure are using simple, well-configured AI to save time and make better decisions right now.
The question is not whether AI works in the African context. It does. The question is whether your organisation has the process discipline to use it well.
AI does not fix broken processes. It accelerates them, good or bad. If your data is unreliable, AI will generate unreliable outputs faster. If your reporting cycle is disconnected from decisions, AI will automate the disconnection.
The organisations that will get the most from AI in the next five years are not the ones with the best infrastructure. They are the ones that have done the boring work of cleaning up their processes first.
What is the messiest internal process in your organisation, and what would have to be true before you could hand it to an AI system?

### Post 63
**Theme: Hiring for data roles in Africa**
Most job descriptions for data roles in Ghana are written by people who have never worked in a data role.
They list every tool ever invented. Python. SQL. Tableau. Power BI. Machine learning. NLP. Big data. The candidate needs five years of experience and should be under 28.
What they actually need is someone who can take a messy spreadsheet, understand what question the business is asking, and produce something a non-technical manager can act on.
That skill is not on most CVs because nobody teaches it explicitly. But it is what separates the analysts who become indispensable from the ones who produce beautiful reports that collect dust.
Technical tools are learnable. Business intuition, communication, and the ability to work with imperfect data without panicking are much harder to train.
If you are hiring for a data role, interview for how someone thinks about a problem, not just whether they know the syntax.
What is one quality you look for in a data hire that does not appear anywhere on a job description?

### Post 64
**Theme: Switching careers into AI and data**

You do not need a computer science degree to work in AI. You need a problem worth solving and the patience to learn what it takes to solve it.

Most people who transition into data and AI roles in Ghana come from somewhere else. Finance. Public health. Communications. Engineering. M&E. And the ones who do well almost always use their previous domain as an advantage, not something to hide.

A public health professional who learns data tools will ask better questions of health data than a data scientist who has to Google what a case fatality rate means.

The combination of domain knowledge and technical skill is genuinely rare. If you have spent years in a field, you already know what the data should and should not be saying. That instinct is worth more than most people realise.

The transition takes time. It is uncomfortable. There will be a period where you feel underqualified in both worlds. That phase does not last.

What field did you come from, and how has it shaped how you think about your work now?

### Post 65
**Theme: Automating the wrong things first**
The first thing most organisations try to automate is the thing that would impress someone in a demo.
They automate the customer-facing chatbot before fixing the internal data that the chatbot will pull from. They automate report generation before agreeing on what the report is supposed to answer. They build the dashboard before deciding who is supposed to look at it and when.
The result is an impressive-looking system that does not actually change anything.
Automation is most valuable when applied to a process that is clearly defined, runs repeatedly, and has obvious quality standards. Not a process that is still being figured out.
Before automating anything, write out the steps by hand first. If you cannot describe the process clearly in plain language, you are not ready to automate it. You will just automate the confusion.
Start with your most boring, repetitive, well-understood task. Not your most exciting one.
What is one task in your workflow that is so routine you could write the instructions for it in ten minutes?

### Post 66
**Theme: Process design before technology**
The worst time to design a process is after you have already bought the software.
Once a system is in place, the path of least resistance is to adapt your process to the tool, not the tool to the process. And most tools were not designed with your organisation's specific context in mind.
This is how organisations end up doing things in strange, inefficient ways that everyone accepts because that is just how the system works.
Good process design happens on paper before any technology is selected. You map what currently happens, where the friction is, where decisions are made, and what information those decisions require. Then you figure out what technology would support that, not replace it.
The organisations that get the best results from technology are almost always the ones that over-invested in process clarity before implementation. The ones that struggle are the ones that skipped that step and hoped the software would sort it out.
Technology does not design processes. People do.

### Post 67
**Theme: The pilot-to-scale problem**
Pilots in African organisations succeed at a rate that does not match the number of things that ever get scaled.
A pilot works because it has a champion, focused resources, a small defined scope, and everyone paying attention to it. Remove any one of those and the results change.
The mistake is assuming that what made the pilot work can be transferred automatically to the full rollout. It usually cannot.
Scaling is not just doing the same thing more times. It means the system has to work without the champion being in every meeting. It has to work when staff turn over. It has to survive budget cuts and competing priorities and the three months when nobody senior is paying attention.
If you want to know whether something will scale, ask what happens when the person who drove the pilot leaves. If the answer is that it probably collapses, you have a people-dependent project, not a system.
Build for the version of your organisation that is having a hard quarter, not the version that is at its best.
What is one initiative in your sector that worked beautifully as a pilot but never made it to scale, and why?

### Post 68
**Theme: Grant reporting vs real M&E**
Most M&E in the development sector is designed to satisfy the funder, not to improve the programme.
This is not a critique of individuals. It is a structural problem. The indicators were negotiated during proposal development, often before anyone knew what would actually be feasible to measure. The reporting schedule matches the donor's review cycle, not the programme's operational rhythm. The audience for the report is the grant officer in London or Washington, not the field team in Tamale.
So the M&E becomes a compliance function. You collect what you agreed to collect, report what was expected, and archive the rest.
Real monitoring asks: what is happening that we did not expect, and what should we do differently? That question rarely appears in a donor report because the answer might complicate the narrative.
The organisations that actually learn from their work have usually built a second, internal M&E track that runs alongside the formal reporting. Less polished. More honest.
That gap should not have to exist. But until funders reward adaptive management over clean narratives, it probably will.
How often does the M&E in your organisation actually change what you do, versus confirming what you already planned to say?

### Post 69
**Theme: The value of structured data**
Unstructured data is not the problem. Unstructured thinking about data is.
A lot of organisations collect information in ways that make it impossible to use later. Notes in free-text fields. Dates formatted differently across spreadsheets. Categories that change between reporting periods because someone renamed them without telling anyone.
None of this feels like a crisis when it is happening. It feels like flexibility. And then twelve months later, someone asks a simple question and it takes two weeks to produce an answer because the data has to be cleaned before it can be analysed.
Structure is not bureaucracy. Structure is the decision you make in advance about how information will be recorded so that future-you does not have to spend a week untangling it.
The best data infrastructure is boring. Consistent field names. Standardised categories. Clear definitions for every indicator. A data dictionary that someone actually maintains. None of this is exciting. All of it is what makes everything else possible.
If you disappeared tomorrow, could someone else use your organisation's data to answer the three most important questions your leadership asks every quarter?
What would have to change for the answer to be yes?
